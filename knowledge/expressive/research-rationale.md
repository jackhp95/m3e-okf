Material 3 Expressive was not a purely aesthetic decision. Google frames it as the
outcome of a large, multi-method research program, and the specific figures matter
because they anchor expressive design to usability rather than to taste.

## Scale of the study

Google reports the system was tuned through **46 separate research studies** involving
**more than 18,000 participants** worldwide, using methods that ranged from surveys and
focus groups (to gauge emotional response) to controlled eye-tracking sessions (to
measure attention).

## The eye-tracking finding

In the eye-tracking work, participants used ten apps in both a baseline Material 3 form
and an M3 Expressive form while wearing eye-tracking glasses. The headline result:
participants **located key UI elements up to four times faster** in the expressive
designs. In one case, asked to "send the email," people's eyes reached the send button
four times faster in the expressive version — a direct measure of how expressive
emphasis guides attention.

## Preference and inclusivity

Two further findings shape the "expressive, well applied" caveat this bundle repeats:

- **Preference.** Well-applied expressive design was preferred across all age groups,
  and that preference was strongest — **up to 87%** — among **18-to-24-year-olds**.
- **Age effects erased.** With expressive designs, Google reports a "dramatic erasure
  of age effects" in fixation times, with users **45 and older performing on par** with
  younger participants. Expressive emphasis, applied well, helps everyone find things
  faster — not only the young.

## Why it constrains, not just licenses

The evidence cuts both ways. It justifies emphasis, motion, and shape as usability
tools — but the qualifier is always **well applied**. The same attention-guiding power
that speeds people up when used sparingly becomes noise when everything shouts at once.
That is the throughline of [applying expressive](/expressive/applying-expressive) and of
the [anti-patterns](/anti-patterns) cluster.
